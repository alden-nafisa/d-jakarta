History of Monas

Learn more about the history of Monas, the National Monument of Indonesia.

Monas: National Pride

Monas (Monumen Nasional) was built to commemorate the struggle for Indonesian independence. Construction began in 1961 and was completed in 1975. The monument stands 132 meters tall and is topped with a flame coated in gold foil, symbolizing the spirit of freedom.

The National Monument (Monas) in Jakarta, Indonesia, is a 132-meter (433-foot) tower built to commemorate the country's struggle for independence. Constructed between 1961 and 1975 under President Sukarno, the monument is topped with a flame-shaped bronze plated in gold, symbolizing the spirit of freedom. The design, by architects Soedarsono and Frederich Silaban, combines elements of Lingga and Yoni, representing masculinity and femininity in Indonesian culture. The base houses a historical museum and a hall displaying the original Declaration of Independence, while an observation deck offers panoramic views of Jakarta.

Monas is a popular tourist attraction and a symbol of national pride, surrounded by a large park known as Merdeka Square. The monument's construction faced delays due to political instability and funding issues, but it was finally completed under President Suharto. The gold-coated flame, weighing over 14 tons, was donated by Indonesian entrepreneurs. Monas serves not only as a historical landmark but also as a venue for national ceremonies and public recreation, reflecting Indonesia's independence and cultural identity.
